Walk into one golf shop and a PXG Lightning Max 10K is $1,199.95. Check another and it's $959.96 — same head, same loft, same year. That $240 difference is not a clearance oddity; on any given day, [our tracker](/drivers) finds a dozen current-model drivers with a spread of $100 or more between Australian retailers.

Here's how to use that to your advantage.

## 1. Never buy from the first store you check

The golf equipment market in Australia is small enough that stores rarely price-match automatically, and big enough that they genuinely compete. The spread between the dearest and cheapest store for the same driver is routinely 10–20%. Checking one extra site is worth more per minute than almost anything else you'll do in golf.

Or skip the legwork: [the drivers table](/drivers) shows every club we track, sorted by how much you save.

## 2. Watch the price, not the RRP

"Was $1,199, now $999" tells you nothing if it's been $999 for six months. What matters is the price *history* — whether today's number is genuinely low for that club. Every club page on LowballGolf shows the price over time, so you can tell a real drop from a permanent "sale".

## 3. Know the model cycle

Most manufacturers release new drivers between January and March. The sharpest price drops on the *outgoing* model usually land two to four months later, when stores clear stock to make room. A one-year-old flagship driver at 30% off is one of the best value plays in golf — the technology difference between model years is smaller than the marketing suggests.

## 4. Set an alert and let the price come to you

If you're not in a hurry, patience is worth real money. Find your driver on LowballGolf, drop your email in the price-alert box, and we'll tell you when it moves. No refreshing five websites at lunch.
